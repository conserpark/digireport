8. 遺蹟參觀報告文

Report on a Visit to Palaeolithic Sites in Korea.

Professor J. Desmond Clark
Department of Anthropology
University of California, Berkeley

In the course of the five days spent in Korea as a guest of the Bureau of Preservation of Cultural Prop­erties and the Department of Archaeology at Seoul National University I visited two Paleolithic sites and a later Pleistocene cave or fissure site with fossil fauna. I also examined and discussed with the ex­cavators the artefacts and fauna recovered from these localities. My impressions from these brief visits and examination of finds in the University Museums is set out below.

Chongokni.

Situated north of Seoul adjacent to the Han Tan River this site was visited on the 17th August in the company of Professor Won-Yong Kim, Director of the excavations since 1979. I was also able to meet and discuss the finds and stratigraph with Dr. Chung and other archaeologists and the geologist, together with Mr. Bae who is supervising the fieldwork. Some four hours were spent examining the sec- tions and the artefacts in the field laboratory.

Discreet assemblages of stone artefacts made mostly from quartz and quartzite and more rarely from hornfels and basalt occur within a red clay which may be in part colluvial and in part a reworked loess, since I understand the mineral composition indicates it is not a product of weathering of the basalt, the heavy minerals being dervied from the Pre-Cambrian gneiss. The clay is some 7 to 8 feet thick the upper 4 feet of which is less red than the lower 3½ feet which is stained by iron oxides. This deposit is very uniform without any visible evidence of bedding or disconformity. Beneath the clay is some 6 or more feet of 'lava gravel' set in a red clay matrix. This is presumably in part colluvial and in part a product of weathering of the basalt. The lava clasts are weathered, sub-angular and have different orientations of the long axis. The basalt bed rock of the plateau on which the site is situated is overlain by a thin deposit of residual clay and in turn lies unconformably on Pre-Cambrian gneiss.

Artefacts are most common in the lower 3½  feet of red clay, but they also occur in the overlying paler coloured clay up to about 1 foot from the surface as well as very rarely in the upper part of the colluvial gravel. The artefacts are in fresh condition and it is most likely that a conjoining study of the pieces in a single concentration with produce a number of pieces that join together.

The section studied suggests that a relatively long period of time may have intervened between the end of gravel formation and the beginning of the accumulation of the red clay. The artefacts occur sometimes on a single horizon distinguished only by the level of the implements, sometimes as a vertically displaced assemblage and sometimes as isolated tools or debitage. The areal dimensions of these con- centrations is generally small perhaps 1 to 2 metres diameter maximum. Sometimes a single large boulder has been flaked and the debitage is lying adjacent to this together with pieces showing retouch and modification. These occurrences have the appearance of single event flaking activity when perhaps a single individual worked the boulder for a brief period of time. The kind of association where many artefacts and tools are concentrated with features over a relatively large area suggestive of a multi- purpose activity site was not seen. The occurrences seen in photographs and slides all have the ap- pearance of relatively brief, one-time workshop areas so that the camping places were presumably situated elsewhere.

The raw material occurs in the form of large boulders and cobbles and can be found in the present river bed and in residual patches of river gravel on a bench some 20 metres above the present stream; the source of the material for the Palaeolithic occupants of the site might have been either of these places.

The artefacts comprise an assemblage with the following components:

Shaped Tools.

A. Heavy Duty. 1 . Bifaces 2. Picks 3. Core Scrapers 4. Polyhedrons 5. Sub-Spheroids

B. Light Duty.  1 . Scrapers both side and end of flakes, flake fragments and chunks. 2. Becs/Borers 3. Notched pieces.

Modified/Edge damaged. 1. Flakes 2. Flake Fragments 3. Chunks 4. Cores 5. Anvils

Unmodified Waste. 1. Flakes 2. Angular waste 3. Cores-single platform; angle; biconical; high-backed discoid.

Bifaces. It is these artefacts that have given the suggestion that the assemblage may belong in the Acheulian Industrial Complex. Forms resembling the Acheulian ficron are present but only rarely (I saw perhaps only one or two). Other rare forms are limande and ovate to discoid. There are also two artefacts, one on a large cortex,end-flake bilaterally trimmed and with convergent cleaver-like bit and the other on an aberrant side-flake that bear resemblances to a cleaver. Other specimens are less regularly retouched and show discontinuous flaking to form irregular bifacial artefacts. A characteristic of most of these specimens is their thickness especially at the butt or proximal end. The primary form is a flattish ellipsoid cobble or a large flake often with cortex struck from large boulder cores. Retouch is by hard hammer and flake scars show deep negative bulbs.

Picks. Two or three artefacts were noted that resemble rostroid and pointed tools having a flat ventral face and steep dorsal retouch along both converging lateral edges so as to form a high backed keel.

Core Scrapers. These with the bifaces are the most characteristic tools in the collections. Many may be single platform cores with one or more areas of the edges showing what appears to be secondary modification to produce a steep, near 90 degree working angle. They may show one, two or more edges worked in this way; they range in dimensions from large to small forms.

Polyhedrons and Sub-Spheroids. Several of these artefacts were noted. They probably represent the ini­tial and intermediate stages towards the true spheroid. The sub-spheroid forms show characteristic bat­tering of the aretes resulting from pounding and use as hammerstones.

Light Duty Tools.Scrapers made on flakes, flake fragments and chunks show straight, convex and den­ticulated retouched edges. Sometimes the retouch is continuous and carefully done but mostly trimming is irregular and casual. These are diminutive forms of the Heavy Duty core scraper and the two classes of tool grade into each other. Several small artefacts show what appear to be opposed notches on an edge to form a shallow bee or borer. A number of pieces show notches that appear to be artificial.

Modified/Edge Damaged. These are mostly flakes and flake fragments that show micro-chipping and crushing that simulate the wear resulting from use. Low power microscope examination might show if this is natural or artificial. Such tools would be useful as knives, while cores showing this modification simulate core-scrapers.

Unmodified Waste

Flakes. These are irregular side and end struck forms having usually a broad inclined striking platform and irregular one or two directional flaking on the dorsal face. Bulbs are prominant on quartzite pieces.

Cores. These are predominantly single-platform examples. Cortex sometimes forms the platform. In addition angle cores (where flakes are struck alternately from both faces of a single edge); also noted were two biconical cores and one high-backed discoid core with radial flaking.

Technology.

Hard hammer technique such as is typical of Lower Palaeolithic assemblages appears to be universal­ly used and no evidence of the Levallois method was seen. Informality is the keynote of the industry. The most characteristic tools are large and small artefacts with steep scraping edges resembling some of the Australian and East African forms of chopper. Edge damaged flakes do not appear to be uncommon and the bifaces and pick forms are a special feature. This informality makes classification of the industry dif­ficult by any morphological method and one based on attributes would be much more satisfactory.

Dating.

In the absence of fauna this is not easy. The assemblage technically and typologically bears some resemblance to Early Acheulian assemblages from East Africa. However such informal artefact forms are found over a wide range of time even into the later Pleistocene. The lower age limit appears to be confirm­ed by a K/Ar age of 270,000 yrs B.P. for the basalt lava flow on which the artefact-bearing clay rests. If such an age is confirmed, and new samples have been taken and submitted to Professor Curtis's Laboratory at Berkeley, then the industry can be no older than the late Middle Pleistocene. Allowing for the weathering of the basalt and deposition of the overlying gravels to accumulate for the most part prior to the archaeological occupation the most probable interpretation is that the Chongokni industry may represent a terminal Lower Palaeolithic. It would therefore belong in time to the Last Interglacial period though I saw nothing that would necessarily preclude its being somewhat later.

About 1㎞ downstream I examined briefly with Dr. Chung the stratigraphy at the mouth of a hang­ing valley where a former tributary channel joined the main river. Here the basalt formed a cliff some 30 metres high resting unconformably on the tilted Pre-Cambrian gneiss; more than one basalt flow may be present here. At the top of the cliff the basalt is overlain by a variable thickness of red clay loam. Presumably banked against the basalt cliff, since the contact was obscured, and resting on a bench cut into the gneiss and about 20 metres above river level is an unsorted gravel of quartz, quartzite, lava and hornfels boulders, cobbles and pebbles set in a coarse sand matrix. This gravel probably continues north­wards up the silted channel but is obscured by further red loam which could have been washed from the clay at the top of the sequence. The tributary channel had at some time in the past been incised deep­ly through the basalt and gneiss to some undetermined depth below present river level. The channel so formed was later aggraded with alluvial sediments up to about 15m above the present stream level.

The later sequence of events, therefore, appears to be as follows:

  • Formation of ± 20m terrace gravel of the Han Tan.
  • Deep downcutting of the main stream and tributary channel to below present river level.
  • Aggradation of alluvium to ± 15m.
  • Downcutting of the main river to present level leaving the channel sediments hanging.

Up-stream the Han Tan River flowing S.E. takes a wide sweeping bend to the south and swings back to the west again as it passes the basalt plateau with the archaeological site. It is possible that this is a ater channel of the river and that the abandoned sediment filled valley was at one time the main, or a distributary channel of the Han Tan itself. Crucial questions to answer at this locality are-does the ±20m gravel bench contain artefacts, where in this sequence does the red clay mantle fit, is it older or younger than this gravel, might it date to the period of deep downcutting?

Conclusions. These must of the course remain tentative until a detailed study of the artefact assemblages is complete, but from the brief observations I was able to make I gained the impression these occurrences represent temporary flaking and workshop areas where a limited set of activities were carried out. Raw matenal all had to be carried in to the site. The Light Duty and Modified artefacts are the most numerous and the industry may be representative official Lower Palaeolithic culture in Korean. In addition, however, the Heavy Duty component (bifaces, picks etc:) appear to be specialized tools manufactured for certain specific activities. The Chongokni occurrences are certainly not a typical Acheulian but they show some affinities with the kinds of assemblages found in Africa at the time range where Heavy Duty core-axes and picks grouped under the term 'Sangoan facies' are believed to be special activity componants of a terminal Lower or early Middle Palaeolithic.

Artefact assemblages are, however, a notoriously unsatisfactory method of dating and it is possible the clays might prove suitable for TL dating; it would certainly be worth a try. In summary this is a very interesting regional industry which will probably be found to share features with some of those of com­parable age from northern and central China. The stratigraphy and inferred sequence of events at the locality 1 km downstream makes it likely that the artefact-bearing clays were deposited prior to the inci­sion of the buried channel which would equate with the global lowering of sea-level during the Last Glacial maximum. If this is so then the artefact-bearing clays probably formed during the warmer climate of the Last Interglacial 125,000 to 80,000 years ago.

Sokchangni

Some two hours were spent in Yonsei University Museum with Professor Pow-key Sohn examining the stone artefacts recovered from this site. The specimens from the lower part of the sequence showed no certain evidence of artificial fracture. The first unquestionable artefacts, made from quartz, quartzite and porphyry, occur about the middle of the section and show resemblances to the Chongokni assemblages. If the artefacts I saw are representative of the assemblage as a whole then the biface com­ponent is not so well developed and the overall impression was that the size dimensions were in general less at Sokchangni. The upper part of the sequence contained flakes and at least one opposed platform core with faceted striking platforms. These arc apparently associated with the characteristic wedge- shaped micro-blade core of the Siberian and Japanese Upper Palaeolithic. Professor Sohn informed me that both the flake and bladelet components belong in the same assemblage. Together with these artefacts occur rarely well made bifacial foliate points. The use of porphyry increased significantly through time. Of special interest is the fact that Professor Sohn has been able to fit back a number of the flakes onto the cores (single-platform) from which they were struck. These conjoining pieces are a clear indica­tion of an undisturbed flaking floor.

When we drove to the site, c. 150kms south of Seoul near Kongju on the Kum River, I was under the impression that the artefacts came from a terrace formation. On visiting the site, however, reached by ferry crossing to the south side of the river, it transpired that the excavations were in alluvial sediments (alternating gravels, sands and clays) the top of which was only some 2-3 metres above the present river level and the main part of the section lay below the bed to a depth of 8-9 metres. These sediments appear to have formed in a buried channel therefore. The excavations were 2 by 2 metre pits and it was necessary to pump the water away in the lower levels resting on the Pre-Cambrian bedrock. It was not possible to see any of these sections since they are destroyed by the annual flooding of the river. Up-slope these alluvial sediments appear to grade into colluvial hillwash which is quite sterile of artefacts. Professor Sohn informed me that he has obtained several radiocarbon dates which range from more than 40,000 BP near the base to ± 20,000 years BP at the top. These sediments and their contained artefacts might be interpreted in two ways. They could represent the sequence accumulated in the buried channel, likely contemporary with the Last Glacial lowering of sea-level. Again since warping is said to have raised the western part of Korea these sediments could be older and date to the time prior to the last marine major regression. In this case the section is presumably composite with at least one unconformity since the assemblage and date within the top levels are clearly in the Upper Palaeolithic.

Yonggul Cave

Situated about 80 kms south-east of Seoul in limestone hill country the site was visited on the 20th August in company of Professor Won-Yong Kim and Mr. Bae. The occurrence is a narrow vertical fissure opening about 3 metres above an artificial terrace at the base of the cliff. There are several other openings and at the terrace level from which a strong continuous stream of cold air is coming; this presumably indicates the existence of a fairly extensive cavern system. The fissure has been excavated by Professor Sohn during the last several years. Since the entrance was locked it was not possible to enter the excava­tion but the general nature and dimensions could be seen through the grille. The fissure appears to be some 2-3 metres wide, 4-5 metres long and some 4 metres or so of red cave earth formed the filling the basal layers being visible through another grille covering a lower opening at terrace level. The few pieces of stone I was shown in the Yonsei Museum showed no obvious evidence of artificial fracture. The selection of fossil mammalian remains that I saw comprises bones of rhino, cave bear, hyaena, lion, deer and other animals, together with aurochs; all are of later Pleistocene age. Typical examples of car­nivore and rodent fracture and alteration of bones is to be seen on these fossils. Absence of comminuted bone fragments, matching tooth puncture marks, grawing, in particular of articular ends of long bones, and the relative completeness of many of the bones are all features more characteristic of Carnivore ac­tivity than human work; it is likely that most of this bone waste results from hyaena activity. A number of toe bones show a consistent pattern of gnawing by a small rodent, which gnawed a roughly circular hole through the bone to get at the marrow. The incisors were small and Professor Sohn suggested the animal might have been Microtis. Though, therefore, I saw no evidence suggesting human activity at the site and the bone assemblage is typical of that associated with carnivore lairs, this does not mean that other sites will not be found that are true human occupation sites. Fissures of the Yonggul type would not have been very suitable as dwelling sites and a search for larger caves with horizontal openings near the base of the hills would be more likely places. Of course subsequent filling and blockage by rock- falls at the entrance might make the existing entrance today look small and insignificant. A regional survey of the limestone hill country in particular where this abutts upon open plains could be expected to produce Upper Palaeolithic occupation sites.

Suggestions for Future Work.

Chongokni. It is clearly essential to ascertain the age of the artefact-bearing clays. Possibly TL dating might provide the answer together with more geomorphological data. For example it is important to know if artefacts occur in the ± 20m bench gravels and in the overlying red clay or whether they are present only in the red clays on the Basalt plateau. The age of 270,000 years BP for the basalt also needs to be confirmed. A quantitative, attribute analysis of the artefacts assemblages in relation to their depth in the clays will show if any developmental trends are present. Future excavation concentrating on opening up bit by bit a larger continuous horizontal area of the occupation surface would show the nature and relationships of the concentrations of flaking waste and tools to each other and give a better overall ap­preciation or density and patterning. A conjoining study of the artefacts in each discreet concentration should show the flaking methods used and the reduction process, and will show which pieces were taken away to be used elsewhere. An experimental study in flaking quartz and quartzite to replicate the Palaeolithic assemblage will show the nature of the debitage to be expected and the relative frequency of the size categories.

Pleistocene Deposits in Limestone Caves and Fissures.

I strongly recommend and a survey of the limestone areas since these offer the best opportunity of finding artefacts and fauna together; it is also relatively certain that a systematic survey of likely areas would produce more carnivore lairs and human habitation sites. Such a survey could be conducted by making use of local informants and college students working in conjunction with a University team. Scien­tists from overseas institutions might also with advantage be invited to participate and here care should be exercised to ensure that those with the necessary expertise to supplement that already available in Korea are invited. Such collaboration could lead to most fruitful results. The most promising areas for human habitation sites can be expected to occur around the lower parts of the hills and facing into the more open country where the game would have been most abundant.

Acknowledgements.

In conclusion I would like to express my very sincere thanks to the Director and staff of the Bureau for Preservation of Cultural Properties, to the Director of the Institute of Cultural Properties, to Professor Won-Yong Kim of Seoul National University, to Professor Pow-key Sohn of Yonsei University, to Mr. Kidong Bae and to all who helped to make my visit such an enjoyable and valuable an occasion. Their kindness in accompanying me on the visits to the sites and the discussions we had there were invaluable. It has been a great pleasure to have been able to be of assistance and I trust that this visit and report will help forward Palaeolithic research in Korea.
