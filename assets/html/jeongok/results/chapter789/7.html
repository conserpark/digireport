7. 英文要約

金元龍

CHONGOKNI: A PROGRESS REPORT

Won-Yong Kim
Department of Archeology 
Seoul National University

INTRODUCTION

The paleolithic site of Chongokni first came to our attention by a discovery of, among others, a quartz­ite handaxe and a cleaver, by Mr. Greg Bowen, then an American soldier stationed at the nearby town of Tongduchon, of April 1978, and his subsequent report of the discovery to Won-yong Kim in the following month. Upon receiving his report along with serveral lithics collected there, Kim visited the site area and collected more artifacts at a new locality. Young-wha Chung of Youngnam University, who was trained under Professor Francois Bordes at University of Bordeaux, was notified the discovery of the site by Kim, and later succeeded in locating a still new locality (cf. below).

In March 1979, Won-yong Kim, under the auspices of the University Museum, Seoul National University, organized an inter-varsity team for a long-term research of the site, which includes a majority of the students of Korean paleolithic archeology and some other specialists of the ielaled fields of Qualei- nary sciences. Following is the list of the names of the members of the team, as well as of their affiliations and specialties.

  • Won-yong Kim, Seoul National University, archeology
  • Chongki Kim, Institute of Cultural Properties, archeology
  • Yong-hun Hwang, Kyunghee University, archeology
  • Young-wha Chung, youngnam University, archeology
  • Mujang Choi, Konguk University, archeology
  • Ingu Kang, Seoul National Museum, archeology
  • Kidong Bae, Seoul National University, archeology
  • Tongwon Park, Seoul National University, geomorphology
  • Sangman Lee, Seoul National University, geology
  • Namki Chung, Seoul National University, biology
  • Igu Kwon, Youngnam University, physical anthropology

After discussions regarding the mode of the proposed long-term research of the site, members agreed to test Locality 2 (cf. below) for the first phase of the research, since it apparently produced most numerous artifacts during the surface surveys and preservation of the original deposits appears to be bet­ter than other localities.

The first campaign took place between March 26 to April 13, 1979. 10×10m square grids were laid over the entire area of Locality 2, and four of them, which crosscut the central part of the area in diagonal direction (i.e., SW to NE; cf. Figure 3), were selected for the initial testing. Nevertheless, due to logistical and time limitations imposed, the actual testing was made on one-quarter area of each grids selected. The first campaign succeeded in providing information on the general stratigraphic nature of the site as well as the stratigraphic positions of artifact horizon(s), however, no special features or faunal remains were discovered until the completion of the fourth campaign (cf. below).

The apparent significance of the nature of the paleolithic industry of the site attracted quite a large number of visitors during the first campaign, both Korean and foreign scholars. Professor Tadashi Nishitani of Kyushu University, Japan, who was staying in Korea as a visiting scholar to Seoul National University, joined the first campaign. Professors Chosuke Serizawa (Tohoku University, Japan, ar­cheology) and Naotsune Watanabe (Tokyo University, Japan, geochronology) were invited to observe the first campaign from March 29 to April 8, 1979.

The second campaign was undertaken for a period of one month, from September 21 to October

20,1979. Funds for the second campaign were provided by the Institute of Cultural Properties. Upon the completion of the second campaign, a negotiation with the government had succeeded in securing the governmental sponsorship for a 10-year project of the Chongokni research starting from 1980, and a semi-permanent structure was built at Locality 2 during the second campaign with governmental sup­ports as a shelter and field office for the campaigners.

The third campaign was taken place for two months from October 3 to December 3, 1980. The fourth campaign lasted for one month in 1981 (October 17 to November 15). The composition of the researchers at this time should have been changed, however, because of the delay in processing and submitting reports on the earlier campaign results by some participants. Also, the National Museum decided to withdraw from the Chongokni project permanently, thus, the fourth campaign was solely ex­ecuted by the Seoul National University team.

This summary is based mainly on data surmised by the Seoul National University team. The entire draft for Korean text regarding the archeology of Chongokni, as well as the drawings of lithics, was prepared by Kidong Bae, followed by reports of specialists of Quaternary sciences on their parts of the research. Also, it includes a report on the background geology and geomorphology, as well as geochronology and stratigraphic nature of the site area by Seonbok Yi, who had been a staff member of the University Museum of Seoul National University until he left for the U.S. for graduate study at Arizona State University in 1980, with a Korean translation by Hui-joon Yi.

Upon reporting the results of the first four campaigns at Chongokni, special words of appreciation should be given to those many anonymous individuals who made this publication possible, especially to the students and the staff members of the Department of Archeology and the University Museum, Seoul National University. Also, Professor Minoru Kojima of Tokyo University has kindly conducted a K-Ar analysis of the basalt sample taken from the site for the benefit of the campaign, which provided the first approximation on the "absolute" chronological limit of the lithic industry of Chongokni (cf. Chapter 6 ).

SITE, CHRONOLOGY AND INDUSTRY

The site of Chongokni is some 50㎞ north of Seoul (38°01'6 "N., 1 27°03 '45 "E.). The Hantan River, a tributary of the Imjin River, makes a U-turn at the site area, surrounding from three directions (E, N, W), Pleistocene terrace (cf. Chpter 5). The terrace exhibits almost flat, nevertheless gently sloping, surface toward the river with elevation between ca. 50 to 60m, ca. 30 to 40m above the normal level of the Hantan River. Although artifacts are occurring at various locations, only the southern portion of the township of Chongokni, where deposits are well preserved and there are few per­manent structures, was designated as "official" site area (total area ca. 2㎢). Thus, further destruction of the current site area by cultivation, construction, and, above all, procurement of red clay for brick pro­duction by brick manufactories there is prevented under governmental protection.

The locations of the 4 officially recognized localities are indicated in Figure 2. Locality 1, the original locus of the 1978 discovery, is situated on the southwestern end of the terrace, to the west of a road leading from the town to the river. Locality 2, identified by Won-yong Kim, lies to the east of the road, also close to the river. Locality 3 was confirmed by Young-wha Chung, and located between Locality 2 and the birck manufactories. Finally, Locality 4 is across the river, opposing Locality 1, which was iden­tified in 1979.

Reports by S. Lee, T. Park and Seonbok Yi on the geology and geoarcheology of chongokni essential­ly summarize the various aspects on the geology of the site area, thus, interested readers should consult with their writings for understanding the general geoarcheological features of the site and its vicinities. It is only necessary here to reiterate the point that the sediments of the site (and the Hantan Basin) appear to be of fluvio-lacustrine origin, although the degree of colluvial processes involved in the basin sedimen­tation would probably as important. Also, both the upper portion of the sediment deposits of reddish brown clay (the so-called "red clay deposits") and the lower one (mostly coarse-grained sand deposits) may include artifact horizons.

Since the area excavated by the Seoul National University team is mainly located on higher elevation, sand deposits were apparently absent or very thin (ca. 20㎝) on the stratigraphic profiles complied by workers from Seoul National University. Well developed sand deposits were found in areas covered by the members of Youngnam and Konguk University. The stratigraphic relationship between the clay deposits of higher elevation and the sand deposits of lower elevation has been a subject of some dispute among the members who participated in the campaigns. During the course of the second campaign, in order to resolve the claims regarding the stratigraphy, a control pit of irregular shape(cf. Figure 3) was tested. From the control pit, time-transgressive relationship between the sand and the clay deposits was clearly seen.

Nevertheless, disputes are far from resolution, since Yong-hun Hwang, who dug a long trench crosscutting the locality in E-W direction, claimed a stratigraphic break between the red clay layers on higher elevation and those on lower elevation near the Datum Point B (cf. Figure 3), arguing that the red clay deposits on lower elevations were essentially retransported from the primary deposits. If such is the case, we may anticipate a rather significant temporal gap between the artifacts collected from the pits excavated by the Seoul National University team and those found in the sandy deposits and/or on the basalt substrate in the central part of the Locality 2.

Although Yong-hun Hwang's position should be examined by detailed microstratigraphic studies and sedimentological analyses, such may not be the case since erosion surface was not well recognized along the proposed 'break line' by others who were with him during the excavation. Further, during the fifth campaign of 1982, repeating of sand and clay layers was identified below sand deposits on lower eleva­tion with artifacts distributing on a thin horizontal plain under a red clay layer with minimal degree of post-depositional disturbances. Not only this discovery supports S. Yi's point that periodic short-term alluviations had been the primary agent for the formatioin of the site, but also it diminishes the plausibility of the redeposition hypothesis. Therefore, at present, there may be no firm stratigraphic evidence to assign the overall assemblages to (at least) two geological episodes which might be significantly apart from each other in temporal sense.

Currently, there are four K-Ar dates available on the age of the basalt substrate, thus, the absolute lower limit of the age of the Chongokni Industry is to a degree controllable. From these dates, we now understand that all the lava flows responsible for the formation of the Chongok Basalt occurred during the Bruhnes Normal Epoch, and the final lava flow directly underlying the sediment deposits with ar­cheological materials should have occurred between ca. 0.4 to 0.15 MY ago, considering the error inter­vals of K-Ar dates (cf. Chapter 5 and 6). More direct data on the formation of the site are, however, currently not available. Two sediment samples were collected by Seonbok Yi during his fieldwork in Korea in 1983 and submitted to a commercial lab for the purpose of thermoluminescence dating, and a piece of organic material, which was collected in 1979 and reported as a possible subfossil of reeds (cf. Chapter 5), was also submitted to the Laboratory of Geochronology, University of Arizona by Yi for the purpose of C-14 dating, applying the particle-accelerating method. If successful, we may have a more 'direct' estimate on the age of the assemblage itself.

During the course of excavations, as indicated earlier, no faunal remains were detected, which may be interpretable in relation to the climatic conditions responsible for the formation of the Red Soil in Korea (i.e., humid and probably warm climatic conditions comparable in some sense to those related to the for­mation of limond rouge in Western European Mediterranean basin) (cf. Chapter 5). Also, playnological analyses were generally futile, although some 100g of sediment samples analysed by Seonbok Yi in 1981 at the Laboratory of Palynology of the Department of Anthropology, Arizona State University, pro­duced a single specimen of extremely deformed pollen grain of Pinus species. Therefore, we lack at pre­sent some 'basic' information which allows to make economic, ecological and behavioral interpretation of the site and the paleolithic culture. The only data bases we currently have are from sedimentological analysis by Sangman Lee and general geological and geomorphological observations of the site and Han­tan Basin by Seonbok Yi as appeared in this report, both of which are rather preliminary in nature.

Nevertheless, some general conclusions can be drawn out from these meager but valuable informa­tion on the ecology of the early hominid occupation of the Hantan Basin, which may serve for guidelines for future researches. First of all, it is apparent that the early hominids occupied riverine environment, possibly exploiting the river-bank gallery forest eco-niche, as is the case of many early African hominid sites. Secondly, while the present geomorphological relief of the surrounding uplands is rather rugged and quite contrastive to the Hantan Basin itself, in spite of the generally low elevation of the former, assuming that the rapid development of the Red Soil and erosional processes had been characteristic since the final lava flow of the Chongok Basalt (cf. Chapter 5), we may project a much sharper geomorphological and ecological contrast between the Hantan Basin and the surrounding uplands during the late Pleistoncene; that is, a vertically diversified ecological niches within a relatively short horizontal distance. If such was the case during the hominid occupation of the area, we may imagine band(s) of paleolithic hunter-gatherers exploiting ecological diversity within a rather narrow, 'closed' ecosystem, thus, may explain the widespread occurrences of artifacts throughout the basin. Thirdly, assuming the above-mentioned ecological conditions and that the same geological processes which enabled the sur­vival of the site area at Chongokni had been working throughout the combined Imjin and Hantan Basin(cf. Chapter 5), we may be able to conduct a study on early hominid settlement pattern by examining lithic assemblages collected at different localities along with other 'contextual' information. Probably the site of Chongokni, judging from its location and other environmental settings, may represent only the southern end of the far-stretching settlement-system of the mobile paleolithic hunter-gatherers which awaits to be discovered.

In that regard, we are not much disappointed by the failure of discovering some dense, vertically compressed 'living-floor-like' artifact concentrations at the site of Chongokni. In fact, the total number of artifacts recovered from the four campaigns by the workers of Seoul National Unversity is rather small-1,082. Probalby the total number of lithics collected by all of the participants in the campaigns in one way or another would be somewhere around 2,000 +, excluding those recovered from the sur­face surveys and later campaigns. Thus, in spite of the impression of extremely high frequency of artifact occurrences on the surface, the actual density of discovery can be said very low.

In general, artifacts were found in the strata as more or less loose vertical accumulations, although one may distinguish 2 or 3 vertical zones of concentration within the clay deposits which may have some temporal significance. Such pattern of artifact dispersal and low artifact frequency per a unit area are ex­plainable when accepting the fluvial origin of red clay deposits (cf. Chapter 5). Any distinct sets of re­mains resulting from seperate occupational episodes should have been disturbed by sedimentation, being mixed with others to a degree, although the relatively 'mint' conditions of many artifacts indicate that the degree of fluvial disturbances might not have been as devastating as one may imagine when consider­ing the general mode of horizontal distribution of artifacts. Thus, it is not at all clear whether statistical tabulations of overall artifacts collected during the excavation would bear directly on behavioral patterns related with site occupation. Probably it is not, and readers should be guided to accept the following descriptions of assemblage as only a summary statistics of the paleolithic implements which may lack any significant behavioral implications.

Among the 1,082 pieces collected, 163 can be classified as 'shaped' (15.1%), while 56 pieces, or 5.2% can doubtlessly be called as 'wastes', when adopting the leiminologies of African school of paleolithic typology (cf. Chapter 1 -1, Table 1 -1). The remainder (863 pieces of 79.8%) are composed of flakes of various sizes many of which seem to carry marks of use and/or modification along the edges. All the stone artifacts from the upper portion of the deposits (i.e., reddish brown clay deposits) are more or less in fresh condition without traces indicating that they were severly 'rolled', however, some of those from the lower portion of the deposits (i.e., sand deposits) show erosional scars and wear marks on the surface, which is an expectable difference considering the differences in the entropy levels involved in the depositional processes between the two distinctive sets of sediment deposits. Major tool types in­clude, again relying on African terminologies, handaxe, cleaver, chopper, chopping-tool, polyhedral, knife, scraper (both side-and end-), burin, and many utilized and utilized flakes in various shapes. This list of tool types is in no way meant to be exhaustive, and more studies should be made on lithics for devising a most appropriate typology for the description of the Chongokni Industry.

Many of the heavy-duty tools are made of large quartzite flakes, some sidestruck, and preserve original surface (cortex) of raw materials, many of which should be water-worn cobbles derived from the Baeguiri Formation underlying the Chongok Basalt (cf. Chapter 5). In spite of the extreme coarseness of the raw materials and seemingly irregular tool shapes, it is still apparent that some of the side-scrapers were deliberately made after certain modal forms, and skillful, systematic exploitation of the extremely unfavorable quality of raw material is evident. Although the reason to turn the site of Chongokni into an archeologists' celebrity in the region is the discovery of 'Acheulian-like' handaxes and that many handaxes were collected during the surface surveys, the total number of handaxes actually recovered from the four seasons of excavations remains to be 5.

There are no significant differences in tool types between those collected from the two sets of sedi­ment deposits, but the ratio of heavy-duty tools against light-duty ones is apparently slightly higher among those collected in sand deposits. When the original shapes of blanks for the manufacture of heavy-duty tools were reconstructed, it seems that diversification is rather a rule, which may indicate the application of the so-called 'block-on-block' technique for the initial procurement and shaping of the raw materials. The application of the side-blow flaking seems to be limited for the manufacture of rather elegantly shaped ones, such as handaxes, cleavers and knives. The predominant flaking angle ranges be­tween 105° and 110° among heavey-duty tools, which in many cases were struck along the cortex plain. Thus, secondary retouch is rather limited in its application, even among the small, light-duty tools. So far, only about 1 5% of the total artifacts indicated the evidence of secondary retouch.

It is a tempting question whether one can formally call the assemblages from Chongokni as classifiable to the so-called 'Acheulian Industry'. In fact, judging from the tool types and assemblage composition, the name of Acheulian is applicable for the description of the assemblages so far identified. However, by calling it having characteristics of the Acheulian, we do not want to make an impression that we would accept the implications and connotations attached to the African and European, as well as other west Asian, Acheulian assemblages in a wholesale mode, nor do we ignore the apparent prob­lems in culture historical approaches in the study of paleolithic archeology. Also, the "absolute" age of the industry is not yet settled. Truly, such a designation would rather hinder than assist archeologists for the study of the dynamics of the regional paleolithic cultures. However, in the absence of standardized classificatory nomenclatures to describe the assemblages with such characteristics as we are seeing from the Chongokni assemblages, we were forced to adopt the name Acheulian for the sake of convenience. Although some common characteristics are found between the Chongokni and the North Chinese assemblages of the so-called 'Kehe-Dingcun Tradition', still there are more problems for us to borrow a terminology from the North Chinese classificatory system. We hope that the current status of the paleolithic archeology in Northeast Asia would be improved one day to allow us to devise an independent typologico-classificatory schemata, which will describe the regional paleolithic assemblages in the most reasonable and parsimonious way.

PROSPECT

Probably the most important significance which had been attached so far to the site of Chongokni since its discovery would be that it provided evidences which enable one to deny categorically the dual phylla theorum so vehemently advocated by Hallam Movius and his followers for such a long time. However, the discussions up to today regarding the nautre of the site and the industry and regarding the nature of the paleolithic cultures in Northeast Asia in general which have appeared in both Asian and Euro-American journals, being inspired by the discovery, have successfully led us to discard the outdated and outmoded notions of cultural parallelism as the most simplistic mode of 'explanation'. Such an 'achievement' would nevertheless be truly meaningful only when alternative, more reasonable and more compelling, explanation(s) is provided through dialectical processes. For that purpose, our work has bare­ly started. Building such explanatory model(s) essentially requires us to have sound methodological frames of references, which Korean archeology does not have yet. Paleolithic archeology in Korea is still in its infancy. Nevertheless, the very adverse conditions of the current situation become the reasons to be hopeful, since more challenging opportunities are offered. The discovery of Chongokni itself had already effectively made us, the community of Korean archeologists, to challenge the authoritative 'theory' on the nature of Asian paleolithic. And we have not yet seen what would be the limits for such challenges. You readers are cordially invited to join us in exploring this valuable oppotunity provided to Korean archeology, and, in that regard, we wish this progress report would make an introduction to future works.
