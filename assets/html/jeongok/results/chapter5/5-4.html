Geoarcheology of Chongokni, Korea1

Seonbok Yi
Department of Anthropology
Arizona State University

Since its discovery in 1978, the paleolithic site of Chongokni(or Chon-gok-ni) has attracted ar­cheological attention as it has produced 'Acheulian-like' handaxes and cleavers in some quantity despite its location in the far eastern end of the Eurasian continental land mass (Kim and Chung 1978, Kim et al. 1981; cf. Yi and Clark 1983). With the financial and administrative support of the Bureau of Cultural Properties of the Republic of Korea, systematic field research has been conducted at the site by Seoul National University Museum teams since 1979. This short report describes some of the salient aspects of the geology and geomorphology of Chongokni, based on published accounts of the 1979-1982 campaigns and field observations made by the writer during the summer of 1983.

The archeological site was named after a small town which lies approximately 50㎞ north of Seoul. Including the original discovery, dense artifact occurrences have been reported at four more or less discrete loci which have been officially identified as four separate sites. However, it is now known that paleolithic artifacts are found at numerous localities in the Hantan River basin over an area about 1 6km long by about 4-5km wide. The distribution of paleolithic remains corresponds roughly to the distribution of the so-called 'red clay' deposits which in this area have developed over the basalt substrate (K. Bae, personal communication; Figure 1). The Hantan River flows west into the Imjin River approximately 7㎞ downstream from the down of Chongokni. From the confluence, the Imjin River travels SSW at an eleva­tion of approximately 20m over a distance of ca. 50㎞ where it debouches into the Yellow Sea at the Bay of Kanghwa2.

The Hantan and Imjin River basin together comprise the southwestern part of the long and narrow valley, current geological views hold that it is type of rift valley; the name of 'Choogaryong Rift Bailey' sula into two distinctive geological zones. While there are conflicting opinions regarding the origin of this valley, current geological views hold that it is a type of rift valley; the name of 'Choogaryong Rift Bailey' has recently been proposed (Lee et al. 1983). In this part of the rift, four major geological episodes are known. The earliest is the formation of the basal rocks of the pre-Cambrian >900 MY) Kyunggi Metamorphic Complex, and subsequent instrusion of biotiticgranodiorite and breccia formation during the Jurassic (180-135 MY). The second stage is marked by Cretaceous (135-65 MY) tectonic act'vities, possibly related to the initial formation of the rift valley. The third stage is characterized by long-term ero­sion of pre-existing rocks beginning in the late Cretaceous and continuing into the Quaternary, and by the deposition of the unconsolidated gravel-sand beds of the Baegeuiri Formation. The final stage is mark­ed by volcanism and lava flows of late Quaternary age which led to formation of basalt plateaux within the valley system. These basalts overlie the Baegeuiri Formation, and are themselves capped by alluvial sedimentation in many areas. Erosional cycles of late Pleistocene and Holocene age complete the geological sequence (Lee et al. 1983).

All of the rocks of volcanic origin formed during the last geological episode were designated the 'Chongok Basalt' by Lee and others (1983). K-Ar3 and paleomagnetic determinations made on the Chongok Basalt suggest formation during the Bruhnes Normal Epoch (ca. .70 MY-present) (Lee et al. 1983). It has been suggested that at least 12 separate lava flows are responsible for the formation of the Chongok Basalt; however, only 2 or 3 flows with total thickness of 1 5 to 20m were identified within the area shown in Figure 1 . Hominid occupation of the region appears to have begun not long after forma­tion of the basalt plateaux, and apparently continued uninterrupted throughout the entire period of Pleistocene alluvial sedimentation. Large scale downcutting of the stream which ultimately led to the for­mation of the current drainage pattern probably began during or immediately after initial human occupa­tion of the area. Traces of at least two, and possibly as many as four, cycles of stream degradation and aggradation are recognizable throughout the combined Imjin and Hantan River basins (Figure 2).

Since the lava flows took place within the narrow confines of the existing drainage system, the for­mation of the relatively impermeable plateaux should have created a basin of sedimentation, with subse­quent accumulation of sediments of alluvial and colluvial origin (derived from the surrounding uplands). Since 1979, however, it has been known that, where the surface of the basalt forms natural depressions, deposits of apparently lacustrine origin develop to a maximum thickness of ca. 1.5 to 2m. This seems to indicate that the irregular surface of the basalt plateaux was once dotted with small ponds and lakes

immediately after the final lava flow. These lacustrine-like marl deposits are overlain by alluvial beds of various thickness and composition which contain rare plant sub-fossiles (possibly rees?)(M. Choi, personal communication). Depending on the elevation, the oldest artifact horizon is found either within alluvial deposits or on top of the basalt bedrock. In the latter case, artifacts are sometimes found immediately on top of a thin, yellow clay layer which is a decomposition product of the basalt (Kim etal. 1981; Hwang 1982, Chung 1980, Choi 1982).

The 'red clay deposits' refers to a massive alluvial stratum of bright reddish color (5 YR) formed at the top of the alluvial beds as either primary or secondary deposits. The 'red clay' has an exceptionally high clay-size particle content in its composition. Here one or two additional artifact horizon(s) was recognized. Although the record on the development of soil profiles was not carefully kept, the absence of a clear-cut change in color and texture suggests that periodic short-term alluviation produced the deposit. While definitive sedimentological and palynological analyses of samples from excavations are still in preparation, there is an opinion that the 'red clay' is eolian in origin, derived from the surrounding uplands under 'cold and dry' climatic conditions (cf. Bae 1 983). The stratigraphy and mechanical analyses of sediment composition indicates otherwise (I. Sohn, perosnal communication), however, and support the viewpoint expressed above—that the 'red clay' is water-laid.4

That the initial hominid occupation of the area took place soon after the stabilization of the final lava flow is justifiable because (1) the degree of weathering of the basalt below the alluvium prior to homind occupation is not much advanced, and (2) no evidence of unconformity has been observed between any successive sediment layers. In other words, after the cooling of the final lava flow, hominids moved into the area probably following game animals which might have been migrating through the corridor of the rift valley and/or attracted to it by the availability of water from natural ponds and streams formed on the surface of cooled lava. The residues they left there were soon covered by repeated alluviations, and were thus protected from serious disturbance until they were exposed by bulldozers in the late 1970s. However, most, if not all, organic compounds were oxidized under the characteristic climatic conditions responsible for the formation of the Red soil in Korea (Kang 1977, 1979).

Although there is no reason to rule out the possibility of the survival of other open-air paleolithic sites in Korea, it is also apparent that the Hantan River basin represents a unique opportunity for the study of Korean paleolithic archeology. Severe Holocene erosion and alluviation cycles in other parts of the peninsular greatly diminish the likelihood of the discovery of the remains of pre-sapiens open-air sites in primary contexts (Yi, in preparation). The conditions which made the survival of paleolithic occupations in the Hantan River basin possible can be summarized as follows:

1. Formation of the basalt plateaux in a norrow rift valley insured the appearance of a basin of sedimentation in which archeological sites would be preserved with relatively minor post- depositional disturbance;

2. Erosion of the plateaux was confined to the major channel of the present river. The characteristic columnar structure and platy cleavage of the basalt bedrock enabled the larger portion of the site area to remain intact; and

3. The formation of cultural deposits on the top of the basalt plateaux (ca. 20m above the former floodplain) effectively kept the deposits from being subjected to later fluvial disturbances due to changes in the Hantan River base level (i.e., changes in sea level).
